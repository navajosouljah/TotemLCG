The Quick Read

Stocks are at all-time highs on a narrow AI rally, but credit card delinquencies just hit the highest level since 2011 (13.1%), the savings rate remains pinned at 2.6%, crypto dumped 5% overnight, and oil is back above $96 on a four-month Strait of Hormuz blockade. Bonds remain the quiet winner as the Fed holds steady. Drought is worsening across cattle country and the corn belt, setting up higher food prices into 2027.

The S&P 500 cleared 7,600 for the first time on Monday, capping a historic nine-week win streak fueled almost entirely by mega-cap AI names. Tech gained nearly 20% in May alone. But small caps are only barely participating, and the VIX at 16 still signals complacency about real risks underneath. The SpaceX IPO on June 12 will test just how much appetite the market has for risk at these valuations.

The bond market is telling a different story. The Fed held at 3.50–3.75% for the third straight meeting, the yield curve has normalized, and the 30-year is knocking on 5%. For investors looking for income with less downside risk than equities, fixed income is increasingly attractive. Meanwhile, oil prices are re-accelerating — Brent near $97 and WTI at $96 — as Iran peace talks collapsed and the Hormuz chokepoint remains closed.

Housing distress continues to build. Foreclosure filings rose 26% year-over-year in Q1 and bank repossessions surged 42% in April. FHA delinquencies are spiking as pandemic-era forbearance expires. Consumer balance sheets are stretched: credit card delinquencies at 13.1%, subprime auto defaults at a 32-year high, and nearly half of BNPL users paying late — all while the savings rate sits at levels not seen since 2008.

Commodities are mixed but the food story is quietly intensifying. Gold pulled back to $4,457 on profit-taking, but drought across the Southern Plains is forcing cattle herd liquidation and threatening corn yields. In crypto, a sharp Tuesday selloff took Bitcoin below $68K and Solana down to $77, reinforcing that digital assets are not leading risk appetite this cycle.

  • Corn drought risk remains the sleeper story. If summer heat materializes as forecast, grain markets could spike hard and add to grocery-price pressure.

  • Subprime auto delinquencies at a 32-year high and nearly half of BNPL users paying late signals real stress below the surface of record stock prices. This is not a healthy consumer.
  • The Q1 dip in credit card balances ($1.25T from $1.28T in Q4) is seasonal, not structural. Year-over-year balances are still up 5.9% and delinquencies are accelerating.
