Section 01 · Market Thesis

Why now.

The market is telling us two things at the same time. Both matter. Most investors are only listening to one of them.

The first thing the market is saying: asset prices are stretched. As Zak laid out in his excellent May 2026 Market Perspective, current valuation conditions reflect a level of dislocation not seen in prior cycles, including the periods preceding the dot-com correction, the 2008 financial crisis, and the 2020 liquidity event. The Buffett Indicator, which compares total stock market value to U.S. GDP, sat at 230% as of December 31, 2025 and reached 238% in June 2026. Warren Buffett historically called readings approaching 200% a sign of "playing with fire." The Shiller CAPE ratio, which smooths earnings over a 10-year window, was at 42.2 in July 2026, territory only seen before the dot-com peak and the 2021 bubble.

The second thing the market is saying: portfolio balances look strong. The S&P 500 rose 252% from June 2016 to June 2026. Investors see rising 401k statements and feel rich. As Zak named directly in his market perspective, much of the gain reflects monetary expansion rather than value creation. M2 money supply rose 78% over the same period, meaning a meaningful portion of that gain came from monetary expansion, not durable value creation.

The collision

Investors feel wealthy because their balances have grown. The growth has been real in nominal terms. But the same investors are exposed to elevated valuations, monetary distortion, and the assumption that public-market liquidity equals safety. Liquidity is not protection. It means you sell quickly.

State Street's 2026 alternatives outlook described stock markets near all-time highs, elevated valuations of risky assets, record concentration within key equity indices, and high equity-bond correlations. Zak's piece points to the same dynamic: institutional capital is already moving, family offices are increasing alternatives allocations, and the retail investor has not caught up yet.

The strategic question

If a portion of portfolio growth is tied to market liquidity and elevated valuations, what portion of capital should be moved into documented commerce?

This is the question Totem is positioned to answer. Not as a competing investment. As a different category of capital deployment entirely.

The work in this document is the strategic answer to the question above. We are not building a better CD or a better bond. We are building the first place where capital can be deployed into productive U.S. commerce with both principal and returns insured by the largest trade credit insurer in the world.

That asset class did not exist before Totem. It is the reason prospects are skeptical. They are not skeptical of us specifically. They are skeptical of a category they have never heard of. The job over the next 12 months is to make the category real in their minds.

What Totem is not

Totem LCG operates in a fundamentally different corner of capital than traditional private equity. PE firms typically underwrite an equity thesis, ownership in an operating company, where value is realized through growth, margin expansion, multiple expansion, or exit optionality. Totem, by contrast, funds the execution of specific, guaranteed purchase orders for goods committed by large, creditworthy buyers, with performance economically insured. In this context, Totem is not pricing an uncertain enterprise value story. It is financing a contracted transaction with bounded fulfillment risk, anchored to the underlying buyer obligation rather than a long-term performance option.

The structural distinction
Funding execution,
not expectation.

In effect, Totem funds execution while traditional capital funds derivative-like expectation. PE and venture returns are contingent on future operating performance, valuation optionality, and exit outcomes. Totem's returns are structured around the monetization of a specific, insured transaction. The result is a model that is closer to specialty trade finance or insured purchase-order funding: capital deployed against a documented commercial obligation, with risk mitigated by insurance and economics tied to fulfillment, not to speculative enterprise-value appreciation.

Private credit is not a peer. It is a different product.

Private credit, like private equity and venture capital, operates in a similarly expectation-driven space, where investors fund a forward-looking business or cash-flow story rather than a defined, executed transaction. Private credit typically structures cash-flow-based lending or unitranche facilities whose returns depend on the borrower's future operating performance and credit profile. In practice, many private credit portfolios have experienced elevated defaults as those projections fail to materialize. The product is an expectation of success.

Totem, by contrast, is not underwriting a credit-risk or cash-flow story. It is financing a specific, insured delivery obligation. The economics are not aligned with the expectation-based, principal-at-risk model of private credit.

Expectation-based capital
PE / VC / Private Credit

Returns contingent on future operating performance, valuation optionality, exit outcomes, or borrower cash flow. The thesis is a forecast. The investor pays today for a result that may or may not materialize. Defaults track the gap between expectation and reality.

Execution-based capital
Totem LCG

Returns structured around the monetization of a specific, contracted, insured transaction. The buyer obligation is the anchor, not a forecast. Risk is bounded to fulfillment. Insurance covers the gap. The economics are tied to delivery, not to enterprise-value appreciation.

Capital stack and recovery

Where Totem sits if anything goes wrong

Within its program structure, Totem's capital deployment is based on a contractual right to repayment and a defined return tied directly to the financed transaction. It is not a residual claim against the vendor's general assets.

As a matter of financial and credit design, Totem's recovery waterfall is structured so its repayment and agreed profit share are sourced from the transaction proceeds. Any third-party creditor lien rights typically attach only to assets or receivables that remain the vendor's after Totem's contractual entitlements are satisfied. In practice, this means creditors only encumber funds that are due to the vendor, not amounts that have been contractually allocated to Totem under the program. The capital-stack structure puts Totem's claim ahead of vendor-level secured claims.

The reverse benchmark

Categories that ended up reshaping investing always looked invisible first.

The pattern is the same every time. A structural truth exists. The math works. The capital is already moving in small ways. But the broader market has not named the category yet, so most allocators cannot see it. By the time they see it, the early movers have already compounded.

Index Funds
1976 to 1991 · 15 years to take hold

Jack Bogle launched the first retail index fund in 1976. Wall Street called it "Bogle's folly." The idea that you matched the market instead of beating it was treated as either obvious or insulting. Today, index funds hold trillions and are the default allocation for most retail capital.

The category did not exist until someone named it.
Private Credit
2008 to 2024 · 16 years to scale

After the 2008 financial crisis, banks pulled back from middle-market lending. Apollo, Ares, and Blackstone Credit stepped in. For a decade, allocators called it niche. By 2024, private credit had grown into a 1.7 trillion dollar asset class and was treated as a core institutional allocation.

The structural truth was the same the whole time.

Totem fits the same pattern. The structural truth is that contracted, insured, short-cycle commercial deployment exists and works. The proof is the 2,000-plus transactions and the zero default rate. The only thing missing is the name and the story. That is what the next 12 months produce.

The language all of us have become accustomed to using is commerce-backed private funding. Three reasons it works:

"Commerce-backed" tells the prospect real business activity is happening behind their capital. It is not synthetic. It is not market sentiment. It is goods moving to buyers.

02

"Private funding" sits next to private credit and private equity in the prospect's mind, but the "commerce-backed" qualifier separates us. We are not generic alternatives. We are specifically tied to commerce.

03

It is defensible in compliance

The language is descriptive, not promotional. It avoids "investment," "guaranteed," and "risk-free" while still landing the punch. The structure stays compliant. The story stays sharp.

Full-value freight insurance is required on international or ocean shipments. Totem only finances consumable goods that are not for resale. Hard hats, PPE, uniforms, warehouse equipment, medical supplies. The goods are used. The buyer pays.
